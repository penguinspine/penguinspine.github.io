Traditional hypothesis testing is a fundamental statistical method employed to draw conclusions about population parameters by analyzing sample data. The primary objective is to assess whether observed differences or effects are statistically significant or merely due to random variation.


The first step in hypothesis testing is to calculate the test statistic.

Formula $ z = \dfrac{{x̄}-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{{\color{Black} n}}} $

The test is then conducted using the test statistic. The specific actions performed in each method primarily rely on whether the hypothesis test is two-tailed, upper-tailed, or lower-tailed. Just taking a quick look at the alternative hypothesis will reveal the shape. A test with a lower tail is one with a less than sign in the alternative hypothesis; one with an upper tail is one with a larger than sign; and a two-tailed test is one with inequality. Click on > and choose < or = to change from a lower tail test to an upper tail or two-tailed test, respectively.

The critical value in the traditional method is determined using the level of importance ($\alpha$). The value of the test statistic offering an area of $\alpha$ in the lower tail of the test statistic's sampling distribution is known as the critical value in a lower tail test. The value of the test statistic offering an area of $\alpha$ in the upper tail of the test statistic's sampling distribution is known as the crucial value in an upper tail test. The test statistic values that provide areas of $\alpha / 2$ in the lower and higher tail of the test statistic's sampling distribution are known as critical values in a two-tailed test.

Lower Tail Test Upper Tail Test Two-Tailed Test
If $z \leq -z_\alpha$, reject $H_0$. If $z \geq z_\alpha$, reject $H_0$. If $z \leq -z_{\alpha/2}$ or $z \geq z_{\alpha/2}$, reject $H_0$.
If $t \leq -t_\alpha$, reject $H_0$. If $t \geq t_\alpha$, reject $H_0$. If $t \leq -t_{\alpha/2}$ or $t \geq t_{\alpha/2}$, reject $H_0$.

During the process of conducting a hypothesis test, the possibility of reaching an incorrect conclusion always exists, resulting in two distinct errors: Type I Error and Type II Error. Type I Error occurs when the null hypothesis is erroneously rejected despite being true. In an ideal scenario, we would prefer to correctly accept the null hypothesis when it is indeed true. Conversely, Type II Error transpires when the null hypothesis is mistakenly accepted when the alternative hypothesis is true. Ideally, we would like to accurately reject the null hypothesis when the alternative hypothesis holds true.
